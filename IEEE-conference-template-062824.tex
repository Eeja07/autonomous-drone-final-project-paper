	\begin{abstract}
		The advancement of Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) technology has created new opportunities for Search and Rescue (SAR) operations. In Indonesia, where natural disasters occur frequently, there is an urgent need for systems capable of assisting victim detection efficiently in areas that are difficult for humans to access. This study designs and implements an autonomous system on a Holybro S500 drone capable of detecting disaster victims automatically using a Raspberry Pi 5 and a USB webcam. The system integrates a Pixhawk 6C Flight Controller as the main navigation controller and a Raspberry Pi 5 as a companion computer responsible for human detection using a fine-tuned YOLOv8n model and autonomous control through MAVSDK Python via the MAVLink protocol. The research encompasses electrical design with a UBEC 5A-based power regulation for stable edge computing, mechanical design ensuring aerodynamic stability through center-of-gravity optimization, and a multi-threaded software architecture integrating GStreamer-based video acquisition, ONNX-accelerated deep learning inference, and an event-driven state machine for autonomous navigation. Fine-tuning the YOLOv8n model on the COCO2017 person class achieved a precision of 0.809, recall of 0.646, and mAP@50 of 0.757. Field validation demonstrated stable real-time detection at 15 FPS on the Raspberry Pi 5 with the ONNX 320$\times$320 deployment configuration, confirming the system's viability for automated SAR missions.
	\end{abstract}

	\section{Introduction}
	The advancement of Unmanned Aerial Vehicle (UAV) technology has opened significant opportunities for utilization in various fields, including Search and Rescue (SAR) operations. Drones possess the capability to reach areas that are difficult for humans to access, such as mountain slopes, dense forests, or disaster-affected areas with damaged infrastructure, thereby increasing the speed, efficiency, and effectiveness of rescue operations in emergency situations \cite{b1}. The global search and rescue drone market continues to exhibit substantial growth, reflecting increasing recognition of UAV potential in humanitarian operations \cite{b2}. Modern UAVs, particularly rotary-wing quadcopters, offer unique advantages for SAR applications due to their ability to hover in place and perform flexible maneuvers, enabling stable visual observation over specific areas of interest \cite{b8}.
	
	In Indonesia, the need for autonomous drone systems in disaster contexts is increasingly urgent. Based on data from the National Disaster Management Agency (BNPB) in 2024, there were 3,472 disaster events resulting in 540 fatalities. Although the frequency of disasters decreased compared to the previous year, the fatality and damage rates increased by almost 96\%, indicating the limited early detection and rapid victim search systems deployed in the field \cite{b3}. Despite this urgency, adoption of drone technology in humanitarian operations within developing countries remains limited due to technical, regulatory, and institutional barriers \cite{b4}.
	
	Existing autonomous SAR drone systems face several critical challenges that limit their operational effectiveness. First, computational limitations of older single-board computers (e.g., Raspberry Pi 3B+ and 4) result in high inference latency and low frame rates that are insufficient for real-time autonomous decision-making \cite{b5, b6}. Second, power management for edge computing devices on drones remains a largely unaddressed engineering challenge, with voltage instability during peak AI workloads causing system failures. Third, most existing systems rely on general-purpose object detection models without task-specific optimization for aerial human detection scenarios.
	
	This research focuses on designing and implementing an autonomous drone system capable of automatically detecting disaster victims. The system is designed so that the drone can operate without manual control, autonomously navigate to sweep the affected area, detect the presence of humans through visual imagery, and make decisions such as returning to a safe point when a victim has been detected or the battery is critically low. The scope of this work is bounded by the following constraints: (1) single-UAV operation using the Holybro S500 frame with Pixhawk 6C flight controller; (2) edge computing limited to a Raspberry Pi 5 (8GB) in headless Linux configuration; (3) visual detection restricted to RGB camera without gimbal stabilization, targeting only the human class (single-class detection); (4) testing conducted under daylight outdoor conditions with non-fully-occluded targets; and (5) flight controller communication via MAVSDK Python over the MAVLink protocol.
	
	The contributions of this work include: (1) a complete hardware-software co-design for autonomous SAR on a low-cost quadcopter platform, (2) an iterative electrical design methodology that resolves edge-computing power stability challenges on the Raspberry Pi 5, (3) a fine-tuned single-class YOLOv8n model optimized for aerial human detection on resource-constrained hardware, and (4) a multi-threaded software architecture integrating GStreamer video acquisition, ONNX-accelerated inference, and an event-driven state machine with real-time web-based monitoring.
	
	\section{Related Work}
	
	\subsection{Autonomous Search and Rescue Drone}
	Elashaal et al. \cite{b5} developed an autonomous SAR drone system using the Holybro S500 platform with a Pixhawk 2.4.8 flight controller and Raspberry Pi 4. The system employed the YOLOv4-tiny algorithm for real-time human detection, achieving an mAP of 96.44\% on top-view imagery. However, the computational limitations of the Raspberry Pi 4 caused high latency and low frame rates in complex terrain conditions. Furthermore, the flight control integration lacked a comprehensive autonomous state machine, relying instead on pre-programmed waypoint missions without adaptive behavior based on detection results. Field testing was also limited to specific environments such as desert and coastal areas.
	
	\subsection{Real-Time Human Detection for SAR}
	Jayalath and Munasinghe \cite{b6} integrated TensorFlow Fast R-CNN-based human detection with autonomous navigation on a Raspberry Pi 4 using the Okutama-Action dataset. The drone was capable of calculating GPS coordinates of victims based on bounding box positions. Although detection accuracy reached 84.1\%, the processing time of 8 seconds per frame was deemed insufficient for dynamic autonomous missions requiring real-time control loop feedback. The system was also highly dependent on natural lighting conditions, and the two-stage detection architecture (R-CNN) inherently limits throughput on resource-constrained edge devices.
	
	\subsection{Target Detection and Avoidance for UAVs}
	Varatharasan et al. \cite{b7} focused on low-cost hardware efficiency using a Raspberry Pi 3B+ and Intel Movidius NCS2. Their research highlighted the use of MAVSDK API for Offboard control mode, demonstrating the viability of velocity-based setpoint commands for autonomous drone navigation. The advantage lay in system stability on low-power devices; however, depth estimation accuracy remained limited due to reliance on stadiametric rangefinding approaches, and the system required an external neural compute stick rather than performing inference natively on the companion computer.

	\section{Theoretical Background}
	
	\subsection{YOLOv8 Architecture}
	YOLOv8 is a single-stage object detection algorithm based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) that performs detection and classification in a single forward pass \cite{b11}. The eighth generation introduces an anchor-free detection head and a C2f (Cross Stage Partial with two convolutions) module that improves accuracy without sacrificing inference speed. The YOLOv8n (nano) variant, containing approximately 3.2 million parameters, is specifically designed for deployment on edge devices. When exported to the ONNX (Open Neural Network Exchange) format, the model benefits from runtime optimizations including graph fusion and memory layout optimization, enabling efficient inference through OpenCV's DNN backend without requiring GPU acceleration.
	
	\subsection{MAVLink and MAVSDK}
	MAVLink (Micro Air Vehicle Link) is a lightweight binary messaging protocol designed for communication between flight controllers and external systems \cite{b12}. The protocol supports bidirectional exchange of telemetry data, command messages, and mission items with minimal bandwidth overhead. MAVSDK provides a high-level software development kit with Python API bindings that abstract the low-level MAVLink message handling, enabling developers to issue asynchronous navigation commands such as Offboard velocity setpoints (VelocityNedYaw), mission uploads, and telemetry subscriptions \cite{b13}. The combination allows autonomous control logic running on a companion computer to seamlessly command the flight controller while continuously receiving sensor feedback.
	
	\subsection{PX4 Autopilot and Offboard Mode}
	PX4 is an open-source autopilot firmware that manages the flight stack, including EKF2 (Extended Kalman Filter) sensor fusion for state estimation and PID-based attitude and rate controllers for motor output \cite{b16}. The Offboard flight mode enables external companion computers to command the drone's velocity, position, or attitude setpoints at high frequency. PX4 enforces safety constraints by rejecting external commands that violate geofence boundaries or flight envelope limits, and automatically reverting to a failsafe mode (Return-to-Launch or Land) if the companion computer communication stream is interrupted.

	\subsection{Electrical Design}
	The electrical design underwent iterative development to achieve stable computational operations during flight.
	
	\subsubsection{Initial Design and Failure Analysis}
	The initial power design utilized 18650 Li-ion batteries (2S configuration) with XL4015E1/XL4016E1 step-down modules. However, the continuous discharge rate of these batteries could not accommodate transient current spikes during YOLOv8 inference initialization, triggering voltage sag and boot failures. A critical incident occurred when power was supplied through the GPIO pins, which lack OVP/OCP circuits, causing permanent PMIC damage.
	
	\subsubsection{Final UBEC-Based Design}
	The final architecture employs a Hobbywing UBEC 5A step-down module powered by the main LiPo 4S (14.8V) battery \cite{b15}. Power is distributed via an XT60 Y-Splitter: one branch to the PDB for ESC/motors and Pixhawk, the other to the UBEC 5A for the Raspberry Pi 5 and webcam. Fig.~\ref{fig:electrical} illustrates the parallel battery configuration. The data interface between the Raspberry Pi 5 and Pixhawk 6C uses a direct USB connection for MAVLink communication \cite{b12, b13}.

	\subsection{Software Architecture}
	The software architecture integrates three concurrent subsystems operating as a multi-rate system on the Raspberry Pi 5, synchronized through message queue communication patterns to prevent race conditions.
	
	\subsubsection{Visual-to-Control Pipeline}
	The primary data flow handles visual perception and autonomous navigation decisions. The GStreamer thread acquires raw video from the Logitech C922 via \texttt{v4l2src}, applies pixel reduction through a \texttt{tee} element, and distributes frames to both a UDP H.264 stream (\texttt{udpsink}) and the inference pipeline (\texttt{appsink} to frame queue). The YOLO consumer thread retrieves frames from the queue, executes deep learning inference using the ONNX-exported YOLOv8n model at 320$\times$320 resolution, and produces bounding box detections that are placed into a detection queue. The Autonomy Controller consumes detection events, processes them through the current state machine context, and generates VelocityNedYaw navigation vectors transmitted via MAVSDK to the PX4 flight controller for motor actuation.
	
	\subsubsection{Telemetry-to-Frontend Pipeline}
	The MAVSDK Listener subscribes to PX4 telemetry (IMU, GPS, battery) via MAVLink and updates a shared state dictionary. A Flask \& SocketIO server samples this dictionary at 5 Hz and transmits JSON payloads over WebSocket to a React-based dashboard, operating on a read-only principle to ensure zero interference with the control loop.
	
	\subsection{Autonomous Behavior Design}
	The autonomous Scout Mission consists of four phases: (1) System Initialization validating all subsystem readiness, (2) Manual Takeoff followed by pilot-initiated OFFBOARD activation via Skydroid T10, (3) Scout Execution Cycle managed by the event-driven state machine, and (4) mission completion through RTL or failsafe termination.
	
	\subsubsection{State Machine for Victim Search}
	The core search behavior is implemented as an event-driven state machine with four states:
	
	\textbf{SCAN}: The drone performs slow yaw rotation at a hover position to expand the camera's observation area. The human detection module operates continuously during this phase.
	
	\textbf{APPROACH}: Upon confirmed human detection with sufficient confidence, the drone navigates toward the target based on the object's position relative to the camera frame center. Movement is performed incrementally to maintain flight stability.
	
	\textbf{DOCUMENT}: When the target is within documentation range, the system captures visual evidence of the detected victim.
	
	\textbf{RETURN}: After documentation, the drone returns to the initial search position and re-enters the SCAN state to continue searching for additional targets.
	
	Fig.~\ref{fig:state_machine} illustrates the complete state machine transition diagram for the scout mission.

	\subsubsection{Target Validation and Lock}
	The system implements a persistence check that validates detection consistency across multiple consecutive frames before generating a confirmed event. Target lock logic evaluates bounding box area (45\% weight), confidence score (25\%), proximity to frame center (20\%), and inter-frame spatial consistency (10\%). The system maintains focus on a locked target unless a new candidate scores 35\% higher, preventing unstable target switching.
	
	\subsubsection{Failsafe Mechanisms}
	Automatic failsafe responses handle low battery ($<$15\%), telemetry loss, and communication failures by triggering RTL or emergency landing. RC override via the Skydroid T10 allows the pilot to assume manual control at any time.
	
	\section{System Implementation}
	
	\subsection{Hardware Integration}
	The Holybro S500 was assembled with custom integration of the Pixhawk 6C flight controller as the base platform. The Raspberry Pi 5 was mounted using 3D-printed PLA enclosures positioned centrally on the lower frame section to maintain the center of gravity. The Logitech C922 webcam was attached via an adjustable downward-facing mount connected through USB 3.0. XT60 connectors were soldered onto the UBEC 5A module with Y-Splitter for plug-and-play LiPo connectivity. Fig.~\ref{fig:drone_assembly} shows the fully assembled system.

	\subsection{Software Implementation}
	The backend system was developed as a Python-based multi-threaded application running on Raspberry Pi OS configured in full headless mode (SSH-only, no X11/Wayland desktop environment). This configuration was adopted to minimize RAM consumption and eliminate display server overhead that competes with the inference engine for computational resources. The system architecture consists of three primary threads operating concurrently:
	
	\textbf{GStreamer Thread}: Acquires video via \texttt{v4l2src}, splits the stream using a \texttt{tee} element into a UDP H.264 branch (\texttt{udpsink}) and a YOLO inference branch (\texttt{appsink} to frame queue). Camera parameters are locked at initialization via \texttt{v4l2-ctl} \cite{b17}.
	
	\textbf{MAVSDK Thread}: Runs a Python asyncio event loop maintaining MAVLink communication with the Pixhawk 6C. The MAVSDK Listener subscribes to telemetry streams and updates a shared state dictionary. The Autonomy Controller consumes detection events and issues VelocityNedYaw setpoints for navigation control.
	
	\textbf{Flask Thread}: Hosts a REST API with SocketIO WebSocket for the monitoring dashboard, sampling shared state at 5 Hz and providing an MJPEG fallback stream for bandwidth-constrained links.
	
	The YOLOv8n model was fine-tuned using Ultralytics, exported to ONNX, and loaded with OpenCV's DNN module at 320$\times$320 resolution. The YOLO consumer implements a non-blocking queue pattern, always processing the most recent frame. The frontend was built using Vite and React with WebSocket-based reconnection, displaying telemetry, camera feed, detection overlays, and state machine logs.

	\subsection{Field Validation}
	Final validation was conducted through outdoor field testing to observe dynamic system behavior under realistic conditions with human targets positioned at distances of 2--5 meters from the drone's operating altitude. The testing environment consisted of an open field area with natural background elements (vegetation, ground textures, shadows) to evaluate the system's robustness against visual noise sources typical of disaster environments.
	
	The evaluation yielded the following findings across three critical performance dimensions:
	
	\textbf{Detection Stability}: Bounding boxes maintained stable target lock during yaw rotation and approach maneuvers. The persistence check effectively filtered transient false detections from visual artifacts (vegetation, shadows), ensuring only validated events reached the state machine. Target lock maintained focus during the APPROACH phase despite bounding box position shifts from drone movement.
	
	\textbf{Computational Efficiency}: The ONNX YOLOv8n at 320$\times$320 sustained $\sim$15 FPS, providing a $\sim$67 ms control loop update period sufficient for smooth VelocityNedYaw navigation. The GStreamer pipeline maintained stable frame delivery without buffer overflows.
	
	\textbf{False Positive Resilience}: The confidence threshold of 0.224, from F1-Score curve analysis, delivered rapid detection without excessive false positives. The single-class fine-tuning concentrated discriminative capacity on human features, and the combination with persistence check proved a robust strategy balancing sensitivity against specificity.
	
	The integrated system comprising headless OS optimization, UBEC 5A power regulation, GStreamer video pipeline, and the fine-tuned YOLOv8n model successfully met the technical specifications required for an autonomous drone victim search mission system. 	\subsection{Discussion}
	The Raspberry Pi 5 represents a meaningful generational improvement over the Raspberry Pi 4 for edge AI drone applications. While Elashaal et al. \cite{b5} reported low frame rates on RPi 4 with YOLOv4-tiny, our system achieves 15 FPS with YOLOv8n, enabling closed-loop autonomous control. The iterative electrical design revealed that the power delivery interface (GPIO vs. USB-C) is as critical as the power source itself GPIO lacks transient protection, making it unsuitable for AI inference workloads. The single-class fine-tuning strategy, training at 640$\times$640 before deploying at 320$\times$320, proved highly effective for concentrating model capacity on aerial human detection.
	
	\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
%	This study successfully designed, implemented, and validated an autonomous human detection system on a Holybro S500 drone using a Raspberry Pi 5 as the companion computer. The research addressed critical challenges in edge-computing power stability through iterative electrical design, culminating in a UBEC 5A-based architecture that eliminates voltage sag during inference workloads. The multi-threaded software architecture, integrating GStreamer video acquisition, ONNX-accelerated YOLOv8n inference, and an event-driven state machine with four operational states (SCAN, APPROACH, DOCUMENT, RETURN), demonstrates a viable and reproducible approach for automated SAR missions on resource-constrained hardware.
	
	The fine-tuned YOLOv8n model achieved a precision of 0.809, recall of 0.646, and mAP@50 of 0.757 on the person class, while maintaining 15 FPS inference throughput on the Raspberry Pi 5 at 320$\times$320 resolution. Field validation confirmed the system's ability to perform stable detection, target tracking through the persistence check mechanism, and autonomous navigation decisions under realistic outdoor conditions. The monitoring subsystem provided operators with real-time situational awareness through a React-based dashboard accessible over Wi-Fi or LTE connectivity.
	
	The current implementation has several limitations defining future work directions: extending to multi-drone collaborative operations, incorporating infrared/thermal sensors for night operations, adding gimbal stabilization, and conducting testing in actual disaster scenarios. Future research will also explore lightweight architectures beyond YOLOv8n, GPS-stamped victim coordinate reporting for SAR command systems, and edge-optimized runtimes such as TensorRT Lite.
